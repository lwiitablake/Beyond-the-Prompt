BEYOND THE PROMPT

Responsible AI Workflow for Adapting OER/ZTC Course Materials

This workflow guides you through a repeatable, human-centered process for using AI to improve OER and ZTC course materials. Each step shows you what to do and why it matters. Open the sections you need.

Work through the steps in order the first time. With practice, the process becomes faster and more intuitive.

Core principle

AI generates a draft. You make the decisions. Nothing reaches students without your review and judgment.

  • 1. Identify
  • 2. Decide
  • 3. Choose
  • 4. Context
  • 5. Draft
  • 6. Judge
  • 7. Revise

Steps 1–5: Setup and generation

01 Step 1 Identify the Instructional Challenge Identify a specific, observable problem with an existing OER or ZTC course material before involving AI in any way.

What should you do?

Identify a specific, observable problem with an existing OER or ZTC course material before involving AI in any way.

Why this step matters

AI produces output based on what you ask for. If the problem is vague, the output will be generic and may not address what students actually need. A clearly defined problem produces a more useful draft and makes the review process easier because you know exactly what you were trying to solve.

This step also protects against using AI for its own sake. If you cannot name a specific problem, that is a signal to pause before continuing.

How to do this step

Ask yourself one or more of the following:

  • What are students confused about or struggling with in this module?
  • Where do students disengage, skip steps, or ask the same questions repeatedly?
  • What is missing—a learning goal, a clear sequence, or a connection to the assessment?
  • What would make this material more usable without changing what it teaches?
  • Is the problem about clarity, structure, alignment, accessibility, or engagement?

Write it down in one or two sentences before moving to Step 2.

Example: “Students cannot tell what to do first in this module. There is no sequence, no learning goal, and no explanation of why each resource matters.”

Common examples:

  • Students are unsure what to focus on in the OER reading.
  • Assignment instructions are unclear or missing context.
  • The module lacks a clear learning path from start to finish.
  • Students encounter unfamiliar terminology with no support.
  • The connection between the OER content and the assessment is not visible.
  • The module feels like a list of links rather than a learning experience.

Guiding question

What specific problem are we trying to solve?

02 Step 2 Decide Whether AI Is Appropriate Before opening any AI tool, make a deliberate decision about whether AI is the right approach for this specific task.

What should you do?

Before opening any AI tool, make a deliberate decision about whether AI is the right approach for this specific task.

Why this step matters

Responsible AI use includes choosing not to use AI. Not every instructional problem benefits from AI involvement. Some tasks require your disciplinary expertise from the start. Others involve sensitive information that should not be shared with an AI tool. Some tasks are simply faster to complete yourself.

This step prevents AI from becoming a default rather than a deliberate choice.

How to do this step—four questions

Answer all four before continuing. If you answer no to any of them, pause and decide whether to refine your approach or complete the task without AI.

1. Is the instructional challenge clearly defined?

If you cannot describe the problem in one or two sentences, go back to Step 1. A vague problem produces a vague output.

2. Will AI help solve this more effectively or efficiently?

AI works well for drafting, organizing, summarizing, brainstorming, and revising. It works poorly for tasks requiring deep disciplinary judgment, personalized student knowledge, or high-stakes decisions.

If the task requires expertise AI cannot replicate, AI may still help with a first draft—but plan for substantial revision.

3. Can I use AI without sharing sensitive, private, or restricted information?

Do not enter student names, grades, disability or accommodation information, identifiable student work, or proprietary institutional data into a public AI tool.

Use a de-identified or fictional version, use an institutionally approved tool with appropriate data protections, or complete the task without AI.

4. Am I prepared to review and take responsibility for the final product?

AI-assisted materials that reach students are your professional responsibility. If you do not have time to review the output carefully, this is not the right moment to use AI for this task.

Guiding question

Should AI be part of the solution?

03 Step 3 Choose an AI Tool Select the AI tool that best fits the task, your privacy requirements, and your institutional context.

What should you do?

Select the AI tool that best fits the task, your privacy requirements, and your institutional context. Tool choice is itself an instructional decision.

Why this step matters

Different tools have different strengths, privacy protections, account requirements, and institutional approval statuses.

Using the wrong tool—or using a tool without understanding its data practices—can create privacy risks, produce lower-quality outputs, or put you out of compliance with institutional policy.

There is no single best AI tool. The right tool depends on what you are trying to accomplish.

How to do this step—decision guide

Use this guide to match your task to a starting point. You are not required to use the same tool as anyone else.

Guiding question

Which tool fits this task, and why?

04 Step 4 Provide Meaningful Context Determine what information the AI needs to produce a useful, bounded, and appropriate draft.

What should you do?

Before generating anything, determine what information the AI needs to produce a useful, bounded, appropriate draft. Write your prompt with that context included.

Why this step matters

AI generates output based on the information you provide. A generic prompt produces a generic output.

A contextualized prompt produces something much closer to what you actually need and reduces the amount of revision required afterward.

Context also sets boundaries. Telling the AI what not to do—such as not inventing readings, due dates, or institutional policies—reduces the risk of fabricated content.

How to do this step

What to include

  • Course learning outcome: The specific outcome this material is meant to support.
  • Student population: Course level, prior knowledge, typical challenges, and relevant context about who your students are.
  • Course modality: In-person, online, hybrid, or HyFlex.
  • The OER material: A description or excerpt, including the title, source, and license when relevant.
  • Desired output: The specific item you want the AI to produce.
  • Tone and audience level: How formal or conversational the language should be and what reading level is appropriate.
  • Explicit constraints: What the AI should not do.

What to exclude

  • Student names, student ID numbers, or personally identifiable information.
  • Grades, grade distributions, or assessment results.
  • Disability or accommodation information.
  • Identifiable student work or submissions.
  • Proprietary or confidential institutional information.
  • More information than the task actually requires.

Example prompt structure

I am redesigning a module for an online general education course that uses OER materials. Students are mostly non-majors in their first or second year. The learning outcome for this module is [outcome]. The module covers [topic] using [list of OER materials].

Please draft a module overview that includes a student-friendly introduction, the learning outcome in plain language, a recommended sequence for completing the work, and a brief explanation of why each item matters.

Do not invent readings, due dates, policies, or facts. Flag anything you are uncertain about.

Guiding question

What does the AI need to know to produce a useful first draft?

05 Step 5 Generate an AI-Assisted Draft Generate one focused, student-facing support and treat what you receive as a first draft only.

What should you do?

Use your selected tool and prepared context to generate one student-facing support for the OER material. Treat what you receive as a first draft only.

Why this step matters

AI can produce a useful starting point quickly. That starting point can save time and surface organizational approaches or language you might not have considered.

AI does not know your students, your institution, your disciplinary standards, or your instructional intent. Every output requires human review before it is usable.

Generating one focused output rather than asking for multiple things at once makes the review process more manageable and the output more targeted.

How to do this step

Paste your context and prompt into the tool. Read the full output before doing anything else. Do not copy it directly into your course.

Possible outputs to request:

  • Module overview or introduction.
  • Reading guide or annotation scaffold.
  • Discussion prompt.
  • Assignment introduction or framing.
  • Plain-language summary of a complex OER passage.
  • Study guide or review questions.
  • Glossary of key terms.
  • Real-world application prompt connecting OER content to student experience.

Remember: This is a starting point, not a finished product. Move to Step 6 before using anything with students.

Guiding question

What starting point did AI provide, and what does it need before it is usable?

Steps 6–7: Human judgment and revision

06 Step 6 Apply Human Judgment Review the AI-generated draft against six criteria before deciding whether it can be used, revised, or rejected.

What should you do?

Review the AI-generated draft against six criteria before deciding whether it can be used, revised, or rejected. This is the most important step in the workflow.

Nothing reaches students without passing through your professional judgment.

Why this step matters

AI produces fluent, confident-sounding text regardless of whether it is accurate, appropriate, accessible, or aligned with your learning outcomes.

A polished output is not a reliable output. The review step is where your expertise, knowledge of your students, and professional responsibility enter the process.

Skipping or rushing this step is one of the most common ways AI-assisted materials cause problems for students.

How to do this step—six review criteria

1. Accuracy

AI can generate plausible-sounding content that is factually wrong, outdated, or invented. This is called a hallucination.

Check for:

  • Facts, statistics, dates, or figures that were not in your original prompt.
  • Disciplinary terminology used incorrectly or imprecisely.
  • Citations, readings, authors, resources, or links that may not exist.
  • Institutional policies, due dates, or requirements that you did not provide.
  • Oversimplifications that distort the original OER.
  • Claims that sound reasonable but that you cannot verify.

Rule: If you cannot verify it, do not publish it.

2. Accessibility

Accessibility means every student can access, navigate, and understand the material, including students using assistive technology, students with disabilities, multilingual learners, and students using mobile devices.

New to accessibility? Start here.

What is accessibility? Accessibility means designing course materials so that every student can use them, regardless of disability, device, language background, or learning difference.

Public colleges and universities in the United States are required to provide accessible educational materials under laws including ADA Title II and Section 504.

What is WCAG? WCAG stands for Web Content Accessibility Guidelines. It is an international standard for digital accessibility. WCAG 2.1 Level AA is the minimum target for this project.

What is UDL? UDL stands for Universal Design for Learning. It encourages flexible learning experiences designed to reduce barriers from the start.

Practical starting points:

  • Use headings to organize content and support screen-reader navigation.
  • Write descriptive link text instead of “click here.”
  • Check that videos have accurate captions and that audio has an equivalent transcript when needed.
  • Do not use color as the only way to communicate meaning.
  • Write instructions in plain language and number steps when order matters.
  • Contact your institution’s accessibility or disability services office for local guidance.

Check for:

  • Heading structure: Are headings used in a logical order without skipped levels?
  • Reading level: Is the language appropriate for your students?
  • Link text: Do links describe their purpose?
  • Lists and structure: Is information presented in a logical and scannable order?
  • Color and visual cues: Does the content rely on color alone?
  • Media: Are captions or transcripts available and accurate?
  • Plain language: Are complex terms defined?
  • Instructions: Are ordered steps numbered?

3. Privacy

Review how information was handled during generation and whether the output creates new privacy concerns.

Check for:

  • Did the prompt include student names, grades, accommodation details, or other protected information?
  • Could the same output have been produced with de-identified or fictional context?
  • Does the output refer to a specific student, even indirectly?
  • Was the tool institutionally approved?
  • Does your institution have rules about which AI tools may be used?
  • Should students be told that AI helped create the material?

4. Alignment

Alignment means the learning outcome, OER materials, activities, and assessment all point in the same direction.

Check for:

  • Does the draft connect clearly to the learning outcome?
  • Does the sequence help students build toward the outcome?
  • Does the activity ask students to engage with the intended learning?
  • Does the draft accurately represent the OER?
  • Would completing the work prepare students for the assessment?
  • Did AI add topics that are outside the module’s scope?

5. OER Considerations

Open Educational Resources have specific licensing, attribution, accessibility, and reuse considerations.

New to OER licensing? Start here.

What is an OER license? Open Educational Resources use licenses that define how they may be used, shared, adapted, and credited.

The most common open licenses are Creative Commons licenses.

  • CC BY: Use, share, and adapt with attribution.
  • CC BY-SA: Adaptations must use the same license.
  • CC BY-NC: No commercial use.
  • CC BY-ND: No adaptations.
  • CC BY-NC-SA: No commercial use; adaptations use the same license.
  • CC BY-NC-ND: No commercial use and no adaptations.

What is attribution? Attribution means crediting the original creator. Include the title, author, source, and license when possible.

Check for:

  • License compatibility: Is the adaptation allowed?
  • Attribution: Is the original source credited correctly?
  • Format accessibility: Is the result accessible and reusable?
  • Accuracy to the source: Did AI change the original meaning?
  • Open values: Did the output introduce paid or restricted resources?

6. Human Judgment

This criterion cannot be reduced to a checklist. Apply your expertise, knowledge of your students, and instructional intent.

Ask yourself:

  • Does this sound like me or like generic AI output?
  • Does the tone fit my students and course?
  • What would I change based on what I know about this class or this point in the term?
  • Does anything feel wrong or incomplete?
  • Are there cultural assumptions or examples that do not reflect my students?
  • Does the language frame students by what they lack rather than the strengths and experiences they bring?
  • Does this reflect my values as an educator?
  • What is missing that only I can provide?

Remember: A professional instinct that something is wrong is worth investigating, even when the output passes other checks.

Guiding question

Would I be comfortable using this with students in its current form?

07 Step 7 Revise Before Students See It Decide what to do with the draft, then act on that decision before any student sees the material.

What should you do?

Based on your review in Step 6, make a deliberate decision about what to do with the draft. Then act on that decision before any student sees the material.

Why this step matters

The revision step is where professional responsibility becomes concrete. AI produced a draft. You decide what happens next.

Publishing AI-assisted materials without review and revision transfers responsibility for accuracy, accessibility, alignment, and appropriateness to a system that cannot hold that responsibility.

How to do this step—four possible outcomes

Use as written

The draft passed all six criteria and reflects your instructional intent. This outcome is less common than it may seem. Most drafts benefit from at least minor revision.

Use after revisions

The draft is a useful foundation but needs editing for accuracy, tone, accessibility, alignment, OER attribution, or instructor voice.

This is the most common outcome. Make the changes, then review again before publishing.

Start over with a better prompt

The draft missed the mark significantly. Return to Step 4, revise your context and prompt, and generate a new draft.

This is not a failure. It is part of the process.

Complete the task without AI

The draft is not usable, or the review revealed that the task requires your expertise from the start.

Choosing not to use AI is a legitimate part of responsible AI use.

Final reflection

Before publishing, ask:

  • What did AI contribute to this material?
  • What did I contribute?
  • Am I confident that what students receive reflects my professional judgment?

Guiding question

What did AI contribute? What did I contribute?
