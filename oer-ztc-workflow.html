BEYOND THE PROMPT

Responsible AI Workflow for Adapting OER/ZTC Course Materials

Use this repeatable, human-centered process to improve Open Educational Resources and zero-textbook-cost course materials with AI.

Each step explains what to do and why it matters. Work through the steps in order the first time. Later, open only the sections you need.

Core principle

AI generates a draft. You make the decisions. Nothing reaches students without your review and judgment.

The seven steps

  1. 1 Identify
  2. 2 Decide
  3. 3 Choose
  4. 4 Context
  5. 5 Draft
  6. 6 Judge
  7. 7 Revise

Steps 1–5: Setup and generation

01 Step 1 Identify the Instructional Challenge Name the specific problem before involving AI.

What should you do?

Identify a specific, observable problem with an existing OER or ZTC course material before involving AI in any way.

Why is this better for learners?

AI produces output based on what you ask for. If the problem is vague, the output will be generic and may not address what students actually need.

A clearly defined problem produces a more useful draft and makes the review easier because you know exactly what you were trying to solve.

This step also protects against using AI simply because it is available. If you cannot name a specific problem, pause before continuing.

How to do this step

Ask one or more of these questions:

  • What are students confused about or struggling with?
  • Where do students skip steps or repeatedly ask the same questions?
  • What is missing: a learning goal, a clear sequence, or a connection to the assessment?
  • What would make the material more usable without changing what it teaches?
  • Is the problem about clarity, structure, alignment, accessibility, or engagement?

Write the problem in one or two sentences before moving to Step 2.

Example: “Students cannot tell what to do first in this module. There is no sequence, no learning goal, and no explanation of why each resource matters.”

See common examples
  • Students are unsure what to focus on in the OER reading.
  • Assignment instructions are unclear or missing context.
  • The module lacks a clear learning path.
  • Students encounter unfamiliar terminology with no support.
  • The connection between the OER content and the assessment is not visible.
  • The module feels like a list of links rather than a learning experience.

Guiding question

What specific problem are we trying to solve?

02 Step 2 Decide Whether AI Is Appropriate Decide whether AI should be part of the solution.

What should you do?

Before opening an AI tool, make a deliberate decision about whether AI is appropriate for this task.

Why is this better for learners?

Responsible AI use includes choosing not to use AI. Some tasks require your disciplinary expertise from the start. Others involve information that should not be shared with an AI tool.

This step protects privacy, keeps professional judgment central, and prevents AI from replacing meaningful teaching or learning.

Ask four questions

Answer all four before continuing. If an answer raises a concern, pause and revise your approach or complete the task without AI.

  1. Is the instructional challenge clearly defined?

    If you cannot describe the problem in one or two sentences, return to Step 1.

  2. Will AI help solve this more effectively or efficiently?

    AI may be useful for drafting, organizing, summarizing, brainstorming, or revising.

    It is less appropriate for high-stakes decisions, deeply contextual judgments, or tasks requiring personalized knowledge of students.

  3. Can I use AI without sharing private or restricted information?

    Do not enter student names, grades, disability or accommodation information, identifiable student work, or confidential institutional data into a public AI tool.

    Use de-identified or fictional information, use an institutionally approved tool, or complete the task without AI.

  4. Am I prepared to review and take responsibility for the result?

    AI-assisted materials that reach students remain your professional responsibility.

    If you do not have time to review the output carefully, do not use AI for the task right now.

Guiding question

Should AI be part of the solution?

03 Step 3 Choose an AI Tool Match the tool to the task, privacy needs, and policy.

What should you do?

Select an AI tool that fits the task, your privacy requirements, and your institutional context.

Why is this better for learners?

Different tools have different strengths, account requirements, data practices, and privacy protections.

Choosing carefully reduces privacy risks, improves the quality of the draft, and helps ensure your work follows institutional policy.

There is no single best tool. The right tool depends on what you need it to do.

Guiding question

Which tool fits this task, and why?

04 Step 4 Provide Meaningful Context Give the AI enough context and clear boundaries.

What should you do?

Decide what information the AI needs to produce a useful, bounded, and appropriate draft. Include that context in your prompt.

Why is this better for learners?

A generic prompt produces a generic output. Meaningful context helps the draft fit your course, students, learning goal, and OER materials.

Clear constraints also reduce fabricated readings, dates, policies, and other details that students could mistake for real course information.

What to include

  • Learning outcome: What the material should help students learn or do.
  • Student context: Course level, prior knowledge, likely challenges, and relevant strengths or experiences.
  • Course format: Online, in-person, hybrid, or HyFlex.
  • OER material: The title, source, license, and relevant excerpt or description.
  • Desired output: The specific resource you want drafted.
  • Tone and audience: Appropriate language, formality, and reading level.
  • Constraints: What the AI must not invent, add, remove, or change.

What to exclude

  • Student names or ID numbers.
  • Grades or identifiable assessment results.
  • Disability or accommodation information.
  • Identifiable student work.
  • Confidential institutional information.
  • Information the task does not need.
See an example prompt structure

I am redesigning a module for an online general education course that uses OER materials. Students are mostly non-majors in their first or second year. The learning outcome is [outcome]. The module covers [topic] using [OER materials].

Draft a module overview that includes a student-friendly introduction, the learning outcome in plain language, a recommended sequence, and a brief explanation of why each item matters.

Do not invent readings, due dates, policies, or facts. Flag anything you are uncertain about.

Guiding question

What does the AI need to know to produce a useful first draft?

05 Step 5 Generate an AI-Assisted Draft Generate one focused output and treat it as a draft.

What should you do?

Use your selected tool and prepared context to generate one focused, student-facing support for the OER material.

Treat what you receive as a first draft only.

Why is this better for learners?

AI can produce a starting point quickly and surface organizational approaches or wording you may not have considered.

Generating one focused item at a time makes review more manageable and reduces the chance that important errors or barriers will be overlooked.

AI does not know your students, discipline, institution, or instructional intent. Every output still requires your review.

How to do this step

Paste your context and prompt into the tool. Read the full response before taking any other action.

Do not copy the output directly into your course.

Possible outputs include:

  • Module overview or introduction.
  • Reading guide or annotation scaffold.
  • Discussion prompt.
  • Assignment introduction or framing.
  • Plain-language summary.
  • Study guide or review questions.
  • Glossary of key terms.
  • Real-world application connected to student experience.

Remember: This is a starting point, not a finished product. Complete Step 6 before using anything with students.

Guiding question

What starting point did AI provide, and what does it still need before it is usable?

Steps 6–7: Human judgment and revision

06 Step 6 Apply Human Judgment Review accuracy, access, privacy, alignment, licensing, and learner impact.

What should you do?

Review the draft against six criteria before deciding whether it can be used, revised, or rejected.

Nothing reaches students without passing through your professional judgment.

Why is this better for learners?

AI produces polished, confident-sounding language even when the content is inaccurate, inaccessible, misaligned, or inappropriate.

This step is where your expertise, knowledge of students, and professional responsibility enter the process.

Review six areas

1. Accuracy Can I verify the content?

AI can generate plausible information that is wrong, outdated, oversimplified, or invented.

Check:

  • Facts, statistics, dates, and figures.
  • Discipline-specific terminology.
  • Citations, readings, authors, and links.
  • Policies, due dates, and course requirements.
  • Claims not included in your source material.
  • Simplifications that change the meaning of the OER.

Rule: If you cannot verify it, do not publish it.

2. Accessibility Can students access, navigate, and understand it?

Accessibility means students can access, navigate, and understand the material across disabilities, devices, and learning contexts.

Check:

  • Are headings used in a logical order?
  • Is the reading level appropriate?
  • Do links describe their purpose?
  • Is information structured in a logical, scannable order?
  • Does the content avoid relying on color alone?
  • Do videos have accurate captions?
  • Is equivalent text available for audio when needed?
  • Are complex terms defined in plain language?
  • Are ordered instructions numbered?
  • Do meaningful images have appropriate alt text?
  • Are data tables identified with captions and proper headers?
New to accessibility? Start here.

Accessibility means designing materials so people can use them regardless of disability, device, language background, or learning difference.

WCAG means Web Content Accessibility Guidelines. WCAG 2.1 Level AA is the minimum standard for this project.

UDL means Universal Design for Learning. UDL encourages flexible learning experiences that reduce barriers from the start.

3. Privacy Was information handled safely?

Check:

  • Did the prompt include names, grades, accommodation information, or protected data?
  • Could fictional or de-identified context have been used instead?
  • Does the output refer to specific students?
  • Was the tool approved by your institution?
  • Does your institution have rules about AI tools or disclosure?
  • Should students be told that AI helped create the material?
4. Alignment Does everything support the learning goal?

Alignment means the learning outcome, OER materials, activities, and assessment point in the same direction.

Check:

  • Does the draft connect to the learning outcome?
  • Does the sequence help students build toward the outcome?
  • Does the activity ask students to practice the intended learning?
  • Does the draft accurately represent the OER?
  • Would students be prepared for the assessment?
  • Did AI introduce material outside the scope of the module?
5. OER Considerations Does the draft preserve open access and licensing?

Check:

  • Is the adaptation permitted by the original license?
  • Is the original source credited correctly?
  • Is the resulting format accessible and reusable?
  • Did AI change the source’s meaning or emphasis?
  • Did the output introduce paid, restricted, or inaccessible resources?
New to OER licensing? Start here.

Open Educational Resources use licenses that explain how the resource may be used, shared, adapted, and credited.

  • CC BY: Adapt and share with attribution.
  • CC BY-SA: Adaptations must use the same license.
  • CC BY-NC: No commercial use.
  • CC BY-ND: No adaptations.
  • CC BY-NC-SA: No commercial use; same license required.
  • CC BY-NC-ND: No commercial use and no adaptations.

Attribution normally includes the title, creator, source, and license.

6. Human Judgment Does this reflect my students, values, and expertise?

Human judgment cannot be reduced to a checklist. Apply your professional expertise, knowledge of students, and instructional intent.

Ask:

  • Does this sound like me or like generic AI output?
  • Does the tone fit this course and these students?
  • What should change based on where students are in the course?
  • Does anything feel wrong, incomplete, or out of place?
  • Are there cultural assumptions that may not reflect students’ experiences?
  • Does the language focus on what students lack rather than what they bring?
  • Are examples varied, relevant, and respectful?
  • Could the wording unnecessarily shame, threaten, or discourage students?
  • Does the resource provide clear choices or support where appropriate without lowering the learning goal?
  • Does this reflect my values as an educator?
  • What is missing that only I can provide?

A professional instinct that something is wrong is worth investigating, even when the output passes the other checks.

Guiding question

Would I be comfortable using this with students in its current form?

07 Step 7 Revise Before Students See It Decide what happens to the draft and act before publishing.

What should you do?

Use your Step 6 review to decide what happens to the draft. Complete the required revisions before any student sees the material.

Why is this better for learners?

Revision is where professional responsibility becomes concrete. AI produced a draft, but you decide what is accurate, accessible, aligned, respectful, and useful.

Publishing without revision transfers responsibility to a system that cannot understand your students or be accountable for the result.

Choose one outcome

Use as written

The draft passes all six review areas and reflects your instructional intent.

This should be less common than minor revision.

Use after revisions

The draft is useful but needs changes for accuracy, accessibility, alignment, attribution, tone, or instructor voice.

Make the changes and review it again.

Start over with a better prompt

The draft has the wrong tone, scope, content, or direction.

Return to Step 4, improve the context and constraints, and generate another draft.

Complete the task without AI

The output is not useful, or the task requires your expertise from the beginning.

Choosing not to use AI is part of responsible AI use.

Final reflection

  • What did AI contribute?
  • What did I contribute?
  • Does the final material reflect my professional judgment?

Guiding question

What did AI contribute? What did I contribute?
