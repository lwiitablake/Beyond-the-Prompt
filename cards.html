RESOURCE CARDS

Information Cards and Decision Cards

Use Information Cards to understand key ideas. Use Decision Cards when you need to choose what to do next.

These card types are related, but they are not a one-to-one set. Some topics need explanation. Some situations need a decision path. Some need both.

How to use these cards

Information Cards explain what something means and why it matters.

Decision Cards help you make a choice and take a next step.

Information Cards

Use these when you need a quick explanation before acting.

Human Judgment

What should you know? AI output still needs expert review.

Why does it matter? Learners deserve accurate, accessible, and context-aware materials.

Accessibility

What should you know? Accessibility means content can be used by people with different disabilities and access needs.

Why does it matter? Accessible design reduces barriers before learners have to ask for help.

Plain Language

What should you know? Plain language means clear, direct wording your audience can understand the first time.

Why does it matter? Clear wording lowers cognitive load and helps busy learners act quickly.

Privacy

What should you know? Privacy means protecting learner information, institutional data, and sensitive context.

Why does it matter? Learners need to trust that their information will not be shared unnecessarily.

Alignment

What should you know? Alignment means the activity, materials, and assessment support the same learning goal.

Why does it matter? Clear alignment helps learners understand why they are doing the work.

Flexible Options

What should you know? Flexible options give learners appropriate ways to access, engage with, or show learning.

Why does it matter? Options can reduce unnecessary barriers while keeping the same learning goal.

Decision Cards

Use these when you need to make a choice. Each card ends with a next step.

Decision Card

Should AI be used here?

What should I do?

Use AI only when the task is low-risk, reviewable, and allowed by policy.

Use AI when...

  • You are brainstorming, outlining, organizing, or revising.
  • You can review the output before learners see it.
  • No private learner information is included.

Do not use AI when...

  • The task involves grades, eligibility, discipline, or accommodations.
  • The task includes private learner information.
  • AI would replace the skill learners are supposed to practice.

Why is this better for learners? It keeps human judgment, privacy, and meaningful learning at the center.

Decision Card

Can I upload this?

What should I do?

Check the content before putting it into any AI tool.

Do not upload if it includes...

  • Names, grades, IDs, messages, accommodations, or submissions.
  • Private institutional information.
  • Copyrighted or licensed content you do not have permission to share.

Safer next step

Remove private details, use a generic example, or use an approved institutional tool.

Why is this better for learners? It protects trust and reduces the risk of exposing information learners did not consent to share.

Decision Card

Is this ready for learners?

What should I do?

Review the material before publishing, sharing, or assigning it.

Check before sharing

  • Can learners tell what to do first?
  • Are expectations clear?
  • Are links, files, images, tables, and media accessible?
  • Does it support the learning goal?
  • Does it reduce confusion or create more of it?

Why is this better for learners? Learners should not have to solve unclear directions, broken access, or hidden expectations on their own.

Decision Card

Can I trust this output?

What should I do?

Treat AI output as a draft, not as a source of truth.

Check carefully if it includes...

  • Facts, statistics, dates, policies, or laws.
  • Citations or source claims.
  • Discipline-specific explanations.
  • Instructions learners will follow.

Safer next step

Verify important claims with reliable sources before using the output.

Why is this better for learners? Confident errors can mislead learners and damage trust.

Decision Card

Does this need documentation?

What should I do?

Document AI use when it affects instructional materials, review processes, or reusable resources.

Document when...

  • AI helped generate, revise, summarize, or evaluate content.
  • The material will be reused or shared.
  • Someone else may need to understand how the resource was made.

Keep it simple

Note what AI helped with, what you changed, and what still needs review.

Why is this better for learners? Documentation supports transparency, accountability, and future improvement.

Decision Card

Should learners use AI for this?

What should I do?

Decide whether AI supports the learning goal or bypasses it.

Allow AI when...

  • It supports brainstorming, feedback, practice, or revision.
  • Learners still do the thinking the task is meant to assess.
  • Expectations and disclosure are clear.

Restrict AI when...

  • AI would complete the core skill for the learner.
  • You cannot tell what the learner understands.

Why is this better for learners? Clear boundaries reduce confusion and help learners use AI without losing the purpose of the work.
