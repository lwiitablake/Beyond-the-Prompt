OER/ZTC COURSE DESIGN

Beyond the Prompt

A scalable model for responsible AI use in course design.

Use AI as a drafting and thinking partner while keeping human judgment, accessibility, privacy, equity, and student learning at the center.

Quick Summary

Responsible AI use is not just about writing better prompts. It is a repeatable workflow: define the problem, gather context, draft carefully, review with human judgment, revise, and document decisions.

The Scalable Workflow

Use this model whenever AI supports OER/ZTC materials, Canvas pages, assignments, assessments, examples, rubrics, or faculty development resources.

Step 1

Define the Problem

Identify the specific course design need before choosing a tool or writing a prompt.

Step 2

Gather Context

Use course outcomes, student needs, assignment purpose, accessibility expectations, and OER/ZTC constraints.

Step 3

Draft With AI

Use AI to generate a first draft, alternative wording, examples, outlines, or checklists.

Step 4

Review Carefully

Check the output for accuracy, accessibility, privacy, bias, alignment, tone, and usefulness.

Step 5

Revise and Document

Make human improvements and note what was changed, why, and what still needs review.

Core Question

Do not ask only, “Can AI do this?” Ask: Should AI help with this, what must a human review, and what could go wrong for students if we miss something?

Information Cards

Information Cards explain key concepts, risks, and review areas. They help users understand what to pay attention to before making a decision.

Clarifying Instructions

AI can help with: simplifying directions, organizing steps, and creating student-friendly wording.

Human review: confirm the instructions match the actual assignment, grading expectations, due dates, and Canvas setup.

Watch for: invented steps, requirements, or submission details.

Accessibility Review

AI can help with: identifying possible barriers, suggesting alt text drafts, and checking for plain language.

Human review: verify heading structure, link purpose, captions, table structure, color contrast, and actual file accessibility.

Watch for: AI claiming something is accessible without actually checking the content.

Creating Examples

AI can help with: drafting sample scenarios, practice questions, and discipline-specific examples.

Human review: check accuracy, cultural assumptions, reading level, relevance, and whether examples reflect diverse student experiences.

Watch for: narrow, stereotyped, or overly generic examples.

OER/ZTC Materials

AI can help with: summarizing open materials, drafting reading guides, and aligning resources to outcomes.

Human review: verify licensing, attribution needs, source accuracy, and whether the resource is actually usable for students.

Watch for: misstated license terms or invented citations.

Rubrics and Feedback

AI can help with: drafting rubric language, feedback stems, and revision-focused comments.

Human review: ensure the rubric matches outcomes, supports learning, and does not punish students for unclear expectations.

Watch for: vague, punitive, or misaligned criteria.

Student-Facing AI Use

AI can help with: brainstorming, practice, revision, and reflection when expectations are clear.

Human review: clarify what students may use AI for, what they must disclose, and what learning still belongs to them.

Watch for: unclear AI policies that create inequity, confusion, or accidental misconduct.

Decision Cards

Decision Cards help users make a judgment. Each card begins with a practical question and ends with a clear next step.

Decision Card

Should AI Be Used Here?

Start with: What are you trying to do?

If the task is brainstorming, outlining, summarizing, or drafting: AI may be appropriate.

If the task affects grades, eligibility, accommodations, discipline, or student records: stop and check policy before using AI.

If student information would be shared: do not use a public AI tool.

Decision: Use AI only when the task is low-risk, reviewable, and does not expose private student information.

Decision Card

Is This Ready for Students?

Check: Can students tell what to do first?

Check: Are directions, due dates, grading expectations, and submission steps clear?

Check: Are links, headings, images, videos, tables, and files accessible?

Check: Does the page support the stated learning outcome?

Decision: Publish only after clarity, accessibility, alignment, and student pathway are reviewed.

Decision Card

Can I Use This AI Output?

If it contains factual claims: verify them with a reliable source.

If it includes citations: check that the sources exist and say what the output claims.

If it changes instructions: compare it to the actual assignment.

If it sounds polished but generic: revise it for your course, students, and context.

Decision: Do not use AI output as-is. Treat it as a draft that requires human revision.

Decision Card

Can I Upload This?

Does it include student names, grades, IDs, accommodations, messages, or submissions? Do not upload it to a public AI tool.

Does it include copyrighted or licensed content? Check the license and institutional policy first.

Is it your own draft or generic course content? AI may be appropriate with review.

Decision: When in doubt, remove private information or use an approved institutional tool.

Decision Card

Does This Need Documentation?

Was AI used to generate, revise, summarize, or evaluate content? Document the use.

Did a human substantially revise the output? Note what changed.

Will others reuse this resource? Include version information and review notes.

Decision: Document AI use when it affects instructional materials, review processes, or reusable resources.

Decision Card

Should Students Use AI for This?

What learning is being assessed? Protect that learning.

Would AI replace the skill students are supposed to practice? Restrict or redesign the task.

Could AI support brainstorming, feedback, revision, or reflection? Allow it with clear boundaries.

Can students explain their process? Require reflection or disclosure.

Decision: Permit AI only when expectations are explicit and the core learning remains visible.

Human Judgment Checklist

  • Is the content accurate?
  • Is it aligned with the course outcomes and assignment purpose?
  • Is it accessible to WCAG 2.1 AA or higher?
  • Does it protect student privacy?
  • Does it avoid stereotypes, bias, or hidden assumptions?
  • Does it make the learning path clearer for students?
  • Would a student know what to do next?

Pause and Apply

Choose one course material you might revise with AI. Before generating anything, write down:

  • What problem am I trying to solve?
  • What student need am I trying to support?
  • What must I review myself before using the output?
